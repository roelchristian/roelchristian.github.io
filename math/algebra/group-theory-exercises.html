This page contains selected problems in Group Theory. Where possible, lemmas/theorems justifying the argument in the solution are linked (most of them, for the sake of convenience, coming from ProofWiki).

Show that all groups of order $4$ are abelian.

Let $G$ be a group of order $4$. By Lagrange’s theorem, all elements in $G$ have order $1$, $2$ or $4$. If an element $a$ has order $4$ then $G$ is cyclic,[1] and thus abelian.[2]

Otherwise suppose all non-identity elements have order $2$. Then $G = \{e, a, b, c\}$ and $a^2 = b^2 = c^2 = e$. By the cancellation law in groups, $ab \neq a$, $ab \neq b$ and $ab \neq e$ and therefore $ab = c$. Thus $(ab)^2 = e$, implying $ab = ba$ and the conclusion follows.

Let $\alpha \in S_n$ be an odd permutation. Show that for any $\beta \in S_n$, $\beta \alpha \beta^{-1}$ is also an odd permutation.

Since $\alpha$ is an odd permutation, it follows that the equation $$ \alpha = \tau_1 \tau_2 \cdots \tau_{m} $$ where each $\tau_i$ is a transposition is true if and only if $m$ is odd. Let $\beta \in S_n$ be an arbitrary permutation whose parity we do not necessarily know. Then if $$ \beta = \tau'_1 \tau'_2 \cdots \tau'_{k} $$ for some $k \in \mathbb{N}$ (the $\tau_i$'s being transpositions), then $\beta^{-1}$ is also a $k$-cycle, i.e., $$ \beta^{-1} = \tau''_1 \tau''_2 \cdots \tau''_{k}, $$ where again each $\tau''_i$ is a transposition. Thus we have $$ \beta\alpha\beta^{-1} = (\tau'_1 \tau'_2 \cdots \tau'_{k})(\tau_1 \tau_2 \cdots \tau_{m})(\tau''_1 \tau''_2 \cdots \tau''_{k}), $$ and therefore $\alpha\beta\alpha^{-1}$ has length $k + m + k = 2k + m$. Since $m$ is odd, there exists a natural number $n$ such that $m = 2n + 1$. Thus $2k + m = 2k + 2n + 1 = 2(k + n) + 1$, which is odd. Therefore $\alpha\beta\alpha^{-1}$ is an odd permutation.
